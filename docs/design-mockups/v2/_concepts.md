# Four palette directions — none of them champagne

Each one commits to **one** accent color with a story behind it. None of them are the current Percy palette. All four ship with light + dark variants.

The point of having a story isn't precious — it's that having a real source for the brand color tells you what to do when you face an edge case. *Why is this red?* "Because it's the color a copy editor's pen makes." That sentence is more useful than #C82B1F.

---

## I — **Inkwell**

**Source.** A fountain pen full of cobalt ink, on cream-laid paper.

**Color soul.** Deep cobalt blue (#1F3FAA) is the only color that exists. Otherwise: paper-warm off-white and ink-warm near-black. No champagne, no greens, no other accents. The blue does *all* the work.

**What it says about Percy.** "We are about writing things down carefully." The voice this implies for copy is direct, considered, slightly literary. Refined without being precious.

**Reference.** Mid-century modernist publishing. Strunk & White. The blueprint hue without the engineering rigidity. Distinct from Stripe purple because it's deeper, less saturated, more "thinking."

**Risk.** Cobalt can read corporate. Need to commit hard to the *cream paper* side or it slides toward generic SaaS-blue.

---

## II — **Press**

**Source.** A Penguin Classic on a desk in 1962. Or a Bloomberg headline printed in red on cream newsprint.

**Color soul.** Pantone 199 / printer's red (#C82B1F) on newsprint cream. Heavy, real ink black. The red appears rarely and confidently — for active states, the brand mark only when warranted, never decorative.

**What it says about Percy.** "We are publishers of organizational truth." Editorial. There's a built-in restraint to it — every page feels like it's been *set*, not arranged. Confidence by way of subtraction.

**Reference.** Penguin paperback covers. The FT's pink replaced with a more confident red. Old-world, but in a way that reads as conviction, not nostalgia.

**Risk.** Red is loud. Has to be used sparingly. If you spray it everywhere it becomes a danger color, not a brand color.

---

## III — **Notebook**

**Source.** An engineer's working notebook. Graph-paper grid faintly visible behind everything. Pencil graphite, fountain ink in a working blue.

**Color soul.** Graphite blue-gray (#3A5070) — a color halfway between pencil lead and Prussian blue. Subtle graph-paper background gives every surface a faint working texture. Pencil grays everywhere.

**What it says about Percy.** "We are the place where you actually work." The most honest of the four — admits this is a tool, not a brochure. Every layout feels like it could have a margin note pencilled in.

**Reference.** Field Notes notebooks. Architect's sketches. The look of a Moleskine but more disciplined. Deliberately *useful*, not pretty.

**Risk.** Subtlety is hard. If the grid texture is too strong → kitsch. If the blue is too desaturated → boring. Has to be confident in its restraint.

---

## IV — **Sodium**

**Source.** A street sign at night. The yellow of a sodium-vapor lamp on warm asphalt.

**Color soul.** Pure warm charcoal (#181816) and sand-cream (#F2EFE7) carry everything. One single sodium yellow (#F5C842) appears *only* on active states, the brand CTA, and live-data indicators. Maybe 2% of pixels.

**What it says about Percy.** "Infrastructure." This is the most "tool" of the four — feels like well-made machinery. Less editorial, more functional. Excellent for the dark-mode-default users (quants, finance, late-night refresh sessions).

**Reference.** GitHub but warmer. Linear but with different conviction. Industrial control panels. The look of something that gets used hard.

**Risk.** Yellow is the hardest accent to use right. Wrong shade slides into "warning sign" or "Best Buy ad." Has to be a specific yellow — slightly orange, a bit dirty, like real sodium light.

## How to read the mockups

Open `index.html` in `v2/`. Use the controls in the top bar to switch between **palette** (I/II/III/IV) and **mode** (light/dark). The dashboard view is identical across all 8 combinations — only the palette changes — so you can compare apples-to-apples.

Then pick whichever feels right *before* reading my opinion below.

---

## My honest read after building all four

**III (Notebook)** is the one I'd build the product around. Every other direction feels like a brand. Notebook feels like a tool. Percy is a tool. The match is too clean to ignore — the graph-paper hint, the pencil-blue, the working-notebook honesty all reinforce what Percy *does* (give you a structured place to work) rather than what it *is* (a presentation tool).

**IV (Sodium)** is the strong second. If you want the app to feel infrastructural and modern in the dark-mode-default tradition, this gets you there without being Linear-cosplay.

**II (Press)** is for if Percy decides to lean *fully* into the "publisher of organizational truth" framing in copy. The visual language has to back the writing — and right now the copy isn't fully there yet, so the visual would lead the brand into something we haven't yet committed to in writing.

**I (Inkwell)** is the safe pick. Beautiful but slightly familiar. Would be my pick if the audience felt more "modern publishing" and less "working analyst."
